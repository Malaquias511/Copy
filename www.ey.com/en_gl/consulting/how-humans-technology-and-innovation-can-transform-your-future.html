        A renewed focus on humans, technology and innovation, and an approach anchored on long-term value, is key to successful transformation.

        It would have been hard to believe, on 1 January, that only a few months later, we would be defining this new decade in terms of pre-pandemic, pandemic and post-pandemic.

        Pre-pandemic, global capital markets were reaching record highs, commodity markets were stable, and consumers were playing a key role in maintaining economic growth with weekend getaways, retail shopping and eating out. As the pandemic took hold, markets fell, people around the world stayed home, global economies slowed significantly and the IMF forecast global GDP declines greater than the 2008 financial crisis.

        When stay-at-home restrictions eased and economies reopened, a new reality emerged. Nearly two-thirds of consumers surveyed in EYâ€™s Future Consumer Index said they will be spending less on vacations, leisure, clothing and big-ticket items shortly after economies re-opened. And in the months after, 52% expected to change the way they shopped and 41% said they would change the products they buy.

        These shifting consumer behaviors have forced companies slow to change to rapidly and fundamentally transform their businesses to survive. For a select few, such as technology companies, communication software providers, home entertainment, social-media companies and e-tailers, it has been an opportunity to excel with their agility to shift business models and address evolving consumer needs. Whether itâ€™s about surviving or thriving, few companies can argue that transformation is essential.

        However, more than being market leaders, we believe successful companies need to go beyond â€” to become market makers. They must look for opportunities to transcend boundaries, generate sustainable growth and consider new measures for long-term value in ways that span customers, employees and society, as well as financials. They must embody a transformative mindset that unlocks human potential, orchestrates engagement, leverages the speed of technology and ignites innovation.

        Companies must look for opportunities to go from market leaders to market makers.

        Investors are looking for purpose and long-term value creation

        In the past, value was defined by profit and loss through consistent quarterly earnings and stock valuations, short-term profit goals, market-share growth and stock repurchases. More recently, corporations have been questioning value definition, as well as the overall purpose of a business. Theyâ€™ve been shifting from being focused purely on shareholders and toward a broader focus on stakeholders.

        The Embankment Project for Inclusive Capitalism (EPIC) brought leaders from across businesses, including asset creators, asset managers and asset owners together to define how to better measure long-term performance for customers, employees, society and financial stakeholders. Notably, environmental, social and governance (ESG)-focused investors are seeing greater returns with the S&P 500 ESG index outperforming the non-ESG index by 3% in the last-twelve months.1

        The pandemic has begun to catalyze the reshaping of businesses and how they are valued. Investors are looking not only for companies that have demonstrated resilience and are disruption-ready, but also to those that have aligned their purpose to long-term value creation.
